False Starts: What Are They and How Do I Stop Them?

Does your baby wake up within an hour after falling asleep at bedtime?

If so, you may be dealing with what's known as a "false start" — when a baby appears to fall asleep at bedtime, only to wake up crying 30–45 minutes later, often seeming wide awake and ready to start the day all over again.

False starts are one of the most frustrating sleep challenges parents face. You've done the whole routine — bath, feed, lullabies, darkness — and just when you thought you were free... the crying starts again.

The Good News?

False starts are extremely common and very fixable. They're usually a sign that something in the sleep environment, schedule, or sleep associations needs to be adjusted. Common causes include:

  • Bedtime being too late (overtiredness causes a cortisol spike that makes it hard to stay asleep)
  • Bedtime being too early (baby isn't tired enough yet)
  • Sleep associations — baby needs a prop (rocking, feeding, pacifier) to fall back asleep
  • An environment issue: too much light, noise, or temperature fluctuation

The fix usually involves tweaking the schedule by 15–30 minutes in either direction, strengthening independent sleep skills, and ensuring the sleep environment is optimized for staying asleep — not just falling asleep.

Want to learn more?

Every baby is different, and so is every false start. If you're struggling with this, a personalized sleep plan can identify the specific cause for your baby and give you a targeted fix — usually within a few nights.

Sleep Environment

The Top Five Sleep Props

What is a Sleep Prop?

A sleep prop is anything your child (or even you) rely on to fall asleep. This could be something you need to settle into sleep at bedtime or to return to sleep if you wake up during the night. As adults, we usually don't have many major sleep props — but we may have small preferences, like the firmness of our pillow or needing to sleep with one foot sticking out.

Common Sleep Props for Babies and Toddlers

Babies and toddlers, however, are still learning how to sleep on their own. They often rely on sleep props like pacifiers, being fed, rocking, or being snuggled to fall asleep. While these props are not inherently bad, when a child becomes overly dependent on them, it can make falling asleep — and staying asleep — much harder.

If they can't fall asleep without their prop, they're likely to stay awake even when exhausted. And if they wake up during the night, they'll want that same prop to get back to sleep.

So, What Can You Do?

Whether your baby is still very young or approaching toddlerhood, there are things you can do to help them sleep more independently without over-relying on sleep props. Start by identifying the main sleep prop your child depends on. For many babies, it's often a pacifier, feeding, or rocking. Then, come up with a plan to gradually reduce or eliminate the need for that prop.

The key here is consistency. Children thrive on routine and knowing what to expect. Stick with your plan as best as you can.

What to Expect?

Expect some resistance in the first few nights. There might be tears (from both you and your little one). But remember, this is an important step in teaching your child to sleep independently — a life skill that will serve them well as they grow. Stick with it, and they'll thank you later!

Sleep Habits

Co-Sleeping: Sleep with Parents or Independently?

As a parent, it's only natural to want to be close to your baby. Co-sleeping can feel like a comforting way to bond and ensure your little one feels safe and secure. However, as a pediatric sleep consultant, I'm here to help you navigate a different path — one that fosters healthy, independent sleep habits for your child in a loving and caring way.

Promotes Healthy Sleep Habits

Babies naturally wake several times during the night. When they're accustomed to sleeping independently, they develop the ability to self-soothe and fall back asleep without external help. This is an essential life skill that supports better rest now and healthy sleep patterns in the future.

In contrast, co-sleeping can make it harder for babies to learn how to self-regulate their sleep. They may come to rely on external factors — like a parent's presence or movement — to drift back to sleep. Teaching independent sleep empowers your baby to navigate these natural nighttime awakenings on their own, leading to longer, more restorative sleep for everyone.

Supports Emotional Development

Helping your baby transition to independent sleep doesn't mean you're abandoning them. On the contrary, you're teaching them to feel secure in their own space and to trust that you'll always respond when they truly need you. These early lessons in independence build confidence and resilience, which are critical to emotional development.

Some parents worry that independent sleep might weaken the parent-child bond, but the opposite is often true. A well-rested child — and a well-rested parent — is better equipped to enjoy quality time together during the day.

Encourages Parent Wellness

Parenting is demanding, and sleep deprivation can take a serious toll on your mental, emotional, and physical health. Co-sleeping often leads to fragmented sleep for parents, as babies' movements, sounds, or sleep cycles disrupt their own. Encouraging your baby to sleep in their own crib can help you reclaim restful sleep and reduce the stress and fatigue that come with disrupted nights.

When parents are well-rested, they're more patient, present, and emotionally available to their children. Prioritizing your own wellness isn't selfish — it's essential for being the best parent you can be.

It is also showing care for your partner by prioritizing time with them apart from your baby. Your baby will grow up and start their own life one day, but the relationship with your partner will remain. Don't forget to make time for each other.

Sleep Skill Building Isn't Just for Babies

When we hear "sleep skill building," most of us picture tiny babies learning to sleep through the night. It is true that sleep skill building is commonly associated with infants, particularly between the ages of 3 to 6 months, as this period is often recommended for initiating sleep skill building methods. But sleep troubles aren't just for the littlest members of the family — they can follow children into their preschool and school years.

In fact, studies show that 20–30% of children experience sleep problems at some point, ranging from difficulty falling asleep to waking frequently at night. Sometimes, these issues stem from unresolved sleep challenges in toddlerhood. Other times, new problems emerge as a child's imagination expands, bringing along vivid dreams or fears.

The truth is, sleep struggles aren't just a childhood thing. Roughly 50–70 million adults in the United States alone deal with sleep disorders. No matter your age, sleep is essential. It's during sleep that our bodies grow, heal, recharge, and solidify memories.

So, let's change the way we think about sleep skill building. It's not just for babies — it's for anyone who needs a little help to unlock the restorative power of sleep. After all, we all deserve to wake up refreshed and ready for the day ahead. Everyone deserves the gift of good sleep.

Newborns

The 5 S's

The 5 S's are a method for soothing crying babies, developed by pediatrician Dr. Harvey Karp. The 5 S's mimic the environment of their mother's womb, making babies feel very calm and safe. The calming reflex only works when it's triggered the right way — so using these techniques in the right order and combination is key.

The 5 S's:

  1. Swaddle — Wrapping your baby snugly mimics the secure feeling of the womb and helps prevent the startle reflex from waking them.
  2. Side or Stomach position — Holding baby on their side or stomach (while awake and supervised) can trigger the calming reflex. Always put baby on their back to sleep.
  3. Shush — A loud "shhhh" sound mimics the constant whooshing sounds baby heard in the womb. White noise machines work great for this.
  4. Swing — Gentle, rhythmic motion mimics the movement baby felt in the womb. Small, fast jiggles (head supported) can be especially effective.
  5. Suck — Sucking on a pacifier or finger activates the calming reflex and helps babies self-soothe.

The 5 S's work best when used together, especially in the early weeks. As your baby grows, they'll need fewer of these tools and will start developing their own self-soothing abilities.

Research

Why Sleep Skill Building?

Sleep skill building might be a controversial topic among new parents, but research continues to demonstrate its significant benefits for both infants and their families. As parents navigate the challenging early months of parenthood, understanding the long-term implications of sleep habits can help inform their decisions.

The Long-Term Impact of Early Sleep Patterns

Research has revealed a compelling connection between early sleep habits and long-term sleep outcomes. Studies indicate that when sleep difficulties aren't addressed in infancy, they don't simply resolve on their own — they can persist for up to three years or even longer. More concerning is the evidence showing that children who experienced sleep problems before six months of age were more likely to continue having sleep difficulties at ages five and ten.

Why Sleep Matters

The importance of quality sleep extends far beyond just getting through the night. Sleep plays a crucial role in:

  • Cognitive development and learning
  • Physical growth (growth hormone is primarily released during sleep)
  • Emotional regulation and behavior
  • Overall well-being of both infant and parents
  • Family dynamics and relationships

The Dual Benefits of Sleep Skill Building

Sleep skill building has emerged as an evidence-based approach that offers advantages for both babies and their caregivers. Research has demonstrated that effective sleep skill building:

  • Improves infant sleep patterns and duration
  • Enhances maternal well-being and mental health
  • Reduces the risk of postnatal depression
  • Improves the overall quality of life for the entire family

Everyone deserves the gift of good sleep. Exhaustion shouldn't be a rite of passage or a badge parents feel forced to wear. Real rest is possible for every family.

7 Reasons To Stay Calm When Babies Cry

This post is inspired by an incredible resource from Janet Lansbury, a widely respected parenting educator. It speaks to something every parent feels deeply: the instinct to immediately stop our baby from crying, no matter what.

The truth is, most parents are not the ones who ignore their babies. Most of us shush, rock, jiggle, use electric swings, washing machines, pacifiers, drive all over the neighborhood, nurse babies for hours on end — anything to avoid even a moment of our baby's discomfort.

But here's what's worth knowing: staying calm when your baby cries is one of the most powerful things you can do for them. Here are seven reasons why:

  1. Your calm is contagious. Babies are incredibly attuned to their caregivers' emotional states. When you stay calm, your nervous system helps regulate theirs.
  2. Crying is communication. Babies have no other way to express hunger, tiredness, discomfort, or overstimulation. It's not a problem to be stopped — it's a message to be understood.
  3. You can't always fix it immediately. And that's okay. Learning that their needs will be met — even if not instantly — builds trust and security.
  4. Panic escalates the situation. When we rush in frantically, babies sense our anxiety and often cry harder.
  5. Calm presence teaches self-regulation. Over time, babies who are met with calm, consistent responses develop better emotional regulation skills themselves.
  6. It protects your mental health. Treating every cry as an emergency is exhausting and unsustainable. Staying grounded helps you parent from a better place.
  7. You are enough. Your steady, loving presence — even without a perfect fix — is exactly what your baby needs most.

Mindset

Why I Stopped Saying "Sleep Training"

I'm a 36-year-old mom of five, and I've been a mom for 13½ years. Before that, I worked with children in various capacities for over 24 years — as a nanny and babysitter for more than 13 of those years — and my college degree was centered around child development. I share all of this not to boast, but to give you some context: I had a lot of experience with children and sleep long before I ever became a certified sleep consultant. I've seen what works and what doesn't. I've learned from both successes and mistakes. And I've worked with many different types of parents across all kinds of situations.

By the time I earned my certification, I already came in with a strong foundation and a lot of opinions about how sleep should be approached with children. What I wasn't prepared for was the unexpected challenge that followed.

The Problem I Didn't See Coming

In the two years since I became certified, I've spent far more time educating the public about what I do — and what I don't do — than I have actually working with families. And please don't misunderstand me: I believe education is essential, and I'm genuinely happy to do it. But there are moments when it feels disheartening, because I know there are families out there who truly need help and aren't reaching out — not because they don't need it, but because they're afraid of what "sleep training" might mean.

And honestly? I get it.

The Term Has Baggage

There are so many methods out there for getting babies and young children to sleep, and not all of them are safe or supportive. I don't blame parents for being hesitant when someone comes along promoting "sleep training" — because depending on who's doing it, that term can mean very different things, some of which are genuinely uncomfortable for families.

So rather than continue spending energy trying to redefine a term that already carries so much weight in people's minds, I've made a decision: I'm retiring "sleep training" from my vocabulary.

Introducing: Sleep Skill Building

Going forward, I'll be calling what I do sleep skill building — because when it's done right, that's exactly what it is.

Think about how we approach other skills our children are learning. When a baby is working on crawling, or a toddler is learning to walk, or a child is figuring out how to ride a bike, we don't just walk away and say "good luck!" We model the skill for them. We stay close to keep them safe. We cheer them on through every wobbly, uncertain attempt until they finally get it.

That's exactly how I want teaching sleep to feel.

Sleep is a skill — and every child has to learn it. But the way they learn it can be safe, gentle, and supported. Mom and dad can be right there, nearby and cheering them on, every step of the way. My goal in working with families always comes back to three things:

  • I want children to be safe.
  • I want the connection between parents and their children to stay strong and secure.
  • I want to give children the time and space they need to develop this skill — just like any other skill they're learning.

A Note on Past Content

You'll still see the term "sleep training" throughout my older blog posts, videos, and social media content — and that's okay. I stand fully behind everything I've shared. But moving forward, you'll hear me say sleep skill building, and now you know exactly why.

If this resonates with you, or if you've been on the fence about reaching out because you weren't sure what working with a sleep consultant actually looks like, I'd love to connect. I promise it's nothing to be afraid of.
